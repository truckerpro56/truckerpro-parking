## The Gap Between Fuel App Checks and Fuel System Optimization

Every experienced driver knows how to check a price app before a fuel stop. Open Trucker Path, scan the upcoming stops, pick the one with the better price. That workflow saves money on individual transactions, and doing it consistently is better than not doing it at all.

But it is not fuel optimization in a systematic sense. A driver checking fuel prices manually on a phone is making one decision at a time, without visibility into how that decision fits into the broader cost picture of the run or the lane. They do not know whether their cost-per-gallon on this corridor is above or below their historical average. They cannot see whether the routing choice they are making today is consistent with the fuel strategy that has worked on this lane before. They cannot automatically compare their card pricing against posted retail at the next seven stops to find the mathematically best option.

A Transportation Management System with fuel optimization capability does all of that — automatically, in real time, integrated with the dispatch workflow. For owner-operators running one truck, it is a significant upgrade from manual price checking. For fleets running five or fifty trucks, it is the difference between having fuel cost data and not having it.

## What TMS Fuel Analytics Actually Shows You

The fuel analytics module in a TMS collects actual fuel purchase data — either through direct fuel card integration or manual entry — and presents it in a way that surfaces actionable patterns over time.

**Cost-per-gallon by corridor and lane.** A TMS tracks what you actually paid per gallon on each lane you run regularly. After a few weeks of data, you can see whether your I-40 runs cost you more or less per gallon than your I-80 runs, whether your average in Oklahoma is better than your average in California, and whether your costs are trending up or down month over month.

**Cost-per-mile fuel efficiency tracking.** Cost-per-mile for fuel is the metric that ties fuel price and fuel efficiency together. A driver who gets better fuel economy can pay a slightly higher per-gallon price and still beat a driver with a worse-burning truck. Your TMS fuel analytics should show both metrics — cost per gallon and cost per mile — so you understand the combined effect of price and efficiency.

**Fuel spend as a percentage of gross revenue.** For owner-operators, the right benchmark for fuel cost is fuel as a percentage of gross, not just absolute dollars. Industry benchmarks for diesel-only fuel costs typically run 22–28% of gross revenue for owner-operators, depending on the lane and current price environment. If your TMS shows you running at 32%, you have a problem to diagnose. If you are at 21%, you are doing something well.

**Anomaly detection.** A TMS that tracks your actual fuel purchases can flag anomalies — a transaction that does not fit your pattern, a stop where you paid significantly above the corridor average, a period where your fuel efficiency dropped sharply. These flags are often the first signal of a problem you would not otherwise catch: a failing injector, a fuel leak, or a fraudulent card transaction.

## Route-Integrated Fuel Planning

The most powerful fuel optimization a TMS provides is not analytics — it is route-integrated fuel planning that recommends specific stops before the run starts, not after you are already driving.

**How route-integrated fuel planning works:**

When a load is dispatched in the TMS, the system knows the origin, destination, total mileage, and available time. It queries fuel prices at truck stops along the route — either through a direct integration with fuel price data providers or through the pricing data from your fuel card network — and calculates the optimal combination of fuel stops that minimizes total fuel cost while satisfying HOS-legal break requirements.

The output is not just "fuel here" but "fuel here, X gallons, at Y cents per gallon, then break here for 30 minutes, then fuel again here" — a complete stop sequence that the driver can follow without making individual price decisions on the road.

For a driver running a regular lane, this removes most of the manual price-checking effort. The TMS does the optimization; the driver follows the sequence and adjusts if conditions change.

**Tankering calculations.** A TMS can calculate whether tankering — carrying extra fuel through a high-price jurisdiction by filling up more than needed in a low-price jurisdiction — is economically justified on a specific run. The calculation requires knowing the price differential, the gallons involved, and the weight penalty. A TMS does this automatically and presents the tankering recommendation as part of the stop plan.

**Dynamic replanning.** Traffic delays, weigh station holds, or a missed fuel stop change the optimal remaining stop sequence. A TMS that integrates ELD data can reoptimize the stop plan in real time based on current HOS hours remaining and actual position, not the theoretical plan from departure.

## Fuel Card Integration: What It Unlocks

Manual fuel tracking — having drivers enter fuel receipts into the TMS at the end of a run — produces data with a lag of hours or days and is subject to entry errors. Fuel card integration changes this.

When your TMS integrates directly with your fuel card network (EFS, Comdata, WEX, Relay, or OTR), every fuel purchase appears in the TMS automatically within minutes of the transaction. The data includes:

- Stop name and location
- Transaction timestamp
- Gallons purchased
- Price per gallon (what was actually paid with the card, not posted retail)
- Total transaction amount
- Odometer reading if reported by the driver

This real-time data feed transforms TMS fuel analytics from a backward-looking reporting tool into a live operational dashboard. You can see whether each driver on your fleet fueled where they were supposed to, whether they paid the card rate, and whether their fuel efficiency is on target — while the load is still moving.

For owner-operators, fuel card integration eliminates manual receipt tracking entirely. The data is in the TMS before you finish fueling, and the end-of-month fuel cost report is generated automatically from the integrated transaction data.

## Fleet Fuel Management for Carriers

For carriers running multiple trucks, TMS fuel analytics aggregates individual driver fuel performance into fleet-level metrics that manual tracking cannot produce practically.

**Driver-to-driver comparison.** A TMS shows which drivers on your fleet are achieving the best fuel economy on comparable lanes and loads. The variation is often significant — a 10–15% fuel efficiency difference between the best and worst performers on the same equipment and lanes is common. Coaching drivers with TMS fuel data as the evidence is more effective than general efficiency reminders.

**Stop compliance tracking.** If your carrier has fuel card contracts with specific chains — and most do — TMS stop compliance tracking shows whether drivers are fueling at contract stops (where they get the negotiated rate) or at out-of-network locations (where they pay retail). Unauthorized out-of-network fueling is a consistent source of above-expected fuel costs for carriers, and TMS data makes it visible.

**Fuel tax reporting (IFTA).** Fuel card integration combined with ELD mileage data produces the raw numbers needed for International Fuel Tax Agreement (IFTA) quarterly reporting automatically. For carriers who manually compile IFTA data from paper fuel receipts and logbook mileage, TMS integration eliminates this workload almost entirely.

**Budget vs. actual fuel cost tracking.** A TMS that integrates fuel cost with load revenue allows carriers to compare budgeted fuel cost per load against actual, by lane, by driver, and by month. This data drives better rate negotiation — if your TMS shows that a specific lane consistently runs 15% above your budgeted fuel cost, you know before your next rate negotiation that the lane needs a price adjustment.

## Choosing a TMS With Fuel Optimization

Not all TMS platforms handle fuel equally. When evaluating a TMS for fuel optimization capability, the key questions are:

**Does it have fuel card integration?** Which cards are supported — EFS, Comdata, WEX, Relay? Integration should be automatic and near-real-time, not manual uploads.

**Does it show cost-per-gallon and cost-per-mile analytics by lane?** These two metrics are the foundation of useful fuel analytics. A TMS that only shows total fuel spend is not optimizing — it is just tracking.

**Does it integrate with ELD data for dynamic HOS-aware stop planning?** A fuel stop plan that does not know your current HOS hours remaining is a plan that can fail mid-route. ELD integration keeps the plan accurate.

**Does it generate IFTA reports?** For carriers required to file IFTA, this is a compliance requirement, not just a nice-to-have. IFTA automation saves significant administrative time.

**Does it support mobile access for drivers?** The stop plan needs to be visible to the driver in the cab, not just on the dispatcher's desktop. A mobile app or driver portal that shows the fuel stop sequence in real time is essential for the plan to actually influence driver behavior.

TMS fuel optimization is not a replacement for driver knowledge and judgment — experienced drivers understand their lanes and conditions in ways that no system fully captures. But it is a force multiplier. A driver who combines their lane knowledge with TMS fuel analytics and integrated stop planning consistently outperforms one who relies on manual price checking alone. For fleet owners, the aggregate savings across multiple trucks, compounding monthly, represent one of the highest-ROI technology investments available in carrier operations.
