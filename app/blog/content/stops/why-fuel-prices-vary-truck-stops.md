## The 30-Cent-Per-Gallon Mystery

Pull off I-40 at any major interchange and you will find diesel priced anywhere from $3.65 to $3.95 at stops within a five-mile radius. The trucks pumping at the more expensive location are not making a mistake — but many are. Understanding why prices diverge this sharply, and the specific mechanisms that drive those differences, is one of the highest-value skills a driver or fleet manager can develop.

The fuel pricing system that produces these disparities is built from at least five distinct layers: wholesale rack pricing, distribution costs, state and provincial taxes, real estate and operating overhead, and individual retailer margin decisions. Each layer adds variability, and the layers compound. The result is a price at the pump that can differ by 30 cents or more over a 50-mile stretch of highway — not because of price gouging, but because the underlying cost structure at each location is genuinely different.

This guide deconstructs each layer so that you can read a fuel price map intelligently and make better buying decisions on every run.

## Layer 1: Wholesale Rack Pricing

The starting point for every gallon of diesel sold at retail is the rack price — the wholesale price at which fuel leaves a bulk storage terminal and enters the distribution chain. Rack prices are published daily by energy data providers like OPIS (Oil Price Information Service) and are publicly available in aggregate form through the US EIA's weekly survey.

Rack prices are not uniform across the country. They vary by terminal location, regional supply conditions, and pipeline access. Gulf Coast terminals — which supply much of the South and Midwest — typically post lower rack prices than terminals in the Northeast or West Coast, which are fed by different pipelines and have different regional supply balances.

The rack price difference between Houston and Albany, New York can be 10–15 cents per gallon on any given day, reflecting the genuine cost difference in getting product from a Gulf Coast refinery to a Northeast terminal versus a Gulf Coast terminal. This geographic rack differential is the foundation on which all retail price variation is built.

**Spot Market Volatility**

Rack prices move daily based on crude oil futures, refinery output, weather events, and geopolitical news. A hurricane in the Gulf of Mexico that disrupts refinery output can push Gulf Coast rack prices up 15 cents in 48 hours. A prolonged cold snap that increases heating oil demand — diesel and heating oil share a refinery slate — can tighten supply and push rack prices up regionally. These spot events create the rapid price swings that make fuel budgeting challenging for fleets.

## Layer 2: State and Provincial Fuel Taxes

State and provincial fuel taxes are the most mechanically straightforward source of price variation, and they are also among the largest. In the United States, the combined federal and state diesel excise tax ranges from under 45 cents per gallon in low-tax states to over 90 cents in California. That is a spread of nearly 50 cents before a single drop of fuel has been priced for retail margin.

**Key State Tax Differentials (2026)**

California leads the country with total diesel taxes exceeding 90 cents per gallon, combining a 36.4-cent state excise tax, a 1.75% state sales tax applied to fuel, and various regulatory fees. Wyoming, New Mexico, and Texas sit at the opposite end, with combined state-plus-federal taxes in the range of 43–52 cents per gallon.

For a practical illustration: a driver topping off a 200-gallon tank in El Paso, Texas pays roughly $80–$100 less in combined taxes than the same driver filling up in Los Angeles, California — before any retail margin or location cost difference is factored in.

**Canadian Provincial Taxes**

In Canada, the equivalent spread runs from Alberta — which has no provincial retail sales tax and moderate fuel levies — to BC, Quebec, and Ontario, which combine provincial fuel taxes, carbon pricing, and in some cases municipal levies. Drivers crossing the Alberta-BC border typically see price jumps of 20–30 cents per litre at the pump, which converts to a very large dollar difference on a full tank.

## Layer 3: Location-Based Operating Costs

After taxes, the next major source of variation is the cost structure of the individual location. Real estate costs, labor rates, environmental compliance, and infrastructure investment all differ significantly by geography, and those costs are passed through to the pump price.

**Urban vs. Rural Pricing**

Truck stops in major metro areas — Los Angeles, Chicago, Dallas, Seattle — face dramatically higher real estate costs than rural counterparts. A major truck stop along I-5 in the Los Angeles Basin occupies land worth millions of dollars per acre. The same footprint in rural Kansas may be worth a fraction of that. Those real estate costs are amortized into the operating overhead that the fuel margin must cover.

Urban locations also face higher labor costs, more extensive regulatory compliance requirements (environmental monitoring, stormwater management, spill containment), and higher utility expenses. The result is that fuel at urban stops tends to run 8–15 cents per gallon above the same chain's rural counterpart on equivalent lanes.

**Competition Clusters vs. Isolation Premium**

When multiple truck stops compete within a few miles of each other — the cluster at major interstate intersections being the clearest example — competitive pressure keeps pricing tighter. The Love's, Pilot, and TA locations within five miles of the I-40/I-35 intersection in Oklahoma City are in direct competition for the same trucks. Any one of them pricing 10 cents above the others will lose volume.

The isolated stop 80 miles from the nearest competitor faces no such pressure. Its customers are trucks that have no reasonable alternative. Unsurprisingly, isolated stops in remote stretches of Nevada, Wyoming, or rural Ontario tend to price 10–20 cents per gallon above network average.

## Layer 4: Branded vs. Unbranded Fuel

Not all diesel is physically the same at the pump, and the distinction between branded and unbranded fuel matters to some fleet operators.

**What "Branded" Means**

Branded diesel — sold under the name of a major refiner (Shell, BP, Chevron, Valero) — typically includes the refiner's proprietary additive package. These additives are designed to exceed the EPA's minimum TOP TIER Diesel standard for injector cleanliness, lubricity, and water dispersion. Branded fuel often carries a higher rack price because of the additive cost and the brand premium.

**Unbranded Fuel**

Unbranded diesel is produced to EPA minimum standards without the proprietary additive package. It meets legal requirements but may not perform identically over time in high-pressure common-rail injection systems. Major truck stop chains that are not affiliated with a specific refiner brand typically sell unbranded fuel at a lower rack cost, which can translate to lower pump prices.

**Does It Matter Operationally?**

For most modern trucks running standard highway diesel, the practical performance difference between branded and unbranded fuel is small. The more meaningful consideration is the additive quality: operators running high-precision injectors in Cummins X15 or Detroit DD15 engines may see injector cleaning benefits from consistently using TOP TIER certified diesel over a high-mileage period.

For fleet managers doing cost-per-mile analysis, the price difference between branded and unbranded at a given stop is worth tracking — but it typically should not override a significant location price advantage.

## Layer 5: Seasonal Price Swings

Diesel prices follow seasonal patterns that are well-documented and partially predictable, driven by refinery maintenance cycles and demand shifts.

**Spring Refinery Maintenance Season**

US refineries typically conduct maintenance shutdowns in February and March, before summer gasoline demand peaks. During these turnarounds, refinery throughput drops and supply tightens. Diesel prices often rise 10–20 cents per gallon nationally from January through April as a result.

**Summer Demand Overlap**

Summer is the peak demand season for long-haul trucking — construction, agriculture, and consumer retail volumes all peak simultaneously. This puts upward pressure on diesel demand coinciding with the period when refineries are ramping back up from maintenance. The net effect is that diesel tends to be most expensive in spring and early summer.

**Winter Heating Oil Competition**

In the Northeast and Midwest, winter heating oil demand competes directly with diesel for the same refinery slate. Cold winters in high-population areas can tighten diesel supply and push regional prices up 5–15 cents per gallon. Diesel fuel also requires cold-flow additives below approximately -15°C (-5°F), adding a modest cost in northern regions.

**Fall Price Trough**

September through November is historically the lowest-price period for diesel in the US, as summer demand subsides and refineries are running at full capacity. Fleets that can accelerate fuel purchases or front-load tankering in this window may capture a seasonal advantage.

## Retail Margin: The Final Layer

After rack price, taxes, and operating costs, the individual truck stop adds its retail margin. This is the layer most visible to drivers but least controllable — it is simply what each location decides to charge above its cost basis.

Margins in the commercial diesel market are typically thin — often 5–12 cents per gallon — but they vary. Stops with high overnight parking demand can cross-subsidize fuel pricing to attract drivers. Stops with high repair bay utilization may price fuel more aggressively to generate traffic. Franchised chain locations must price within brand guidelines but have some local discretion.

The loyalty program discount — the 2–5 cents per gallon you get for being enrolled in myRewards Plus, My Love Rewards, or UltraONE — is coming out of this retail margin layer. The chain is trading a portion of their margin for customer loyalty and volume.

## Reading the Price Map

The practical implication of this five-layer framework is that the price at any given pump is not arbitrary — it is the sum of rack price geography, tax jurisdiction, operating cost structure, fuel brand tier, and retail margin. When you see a 25-cent spread between two stops five miles apart, at least three or four of those five layers are contributing to the difference.

Effective fuel planning means knowing which layers are variable on your routes:

- Identify tax-efficient jurisdictions on your lanes and build your tankering plan around them.
- Use loyalty program pricing and fuel cards to reduce the retail margin layer on every gallon.
- Avoid isolated stops with no competition when alternatives within 20 miles exist.
- Track seasonal patterns on your specific lanes — if you run the same lane every year, your historical cost-per-gallon data is the best predictor of where prices will go this season.
- Check real-time rack pricing data when planning long runs — when national rack prices have moved sharply, the stops that update their prices slowly create temporary arbitrage opportunities.

None of this requires special access or expensive tools. It requires attention to the structure of the market you operate in every day. Over a year of running, a driver who understands fuel pricing can realistically save $3,000–$8,000 compared to one who does not — not through luck, but through systematically buying in the right layers at the right times.
