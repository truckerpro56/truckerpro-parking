## Fuel Is the Number You Can Actually Move

For a 20-truck carrier running 120,000 miles per truck per year at an average of 6.5 MPG, annual diesel consumption is approximately 369,000 gallons. At $3.90 per gallon — a rough 2026 national average — that is $1.44 million in annual fuel. A 15% reduction in fuel cost is $216,000 per year. Against a typical small carrier's net margin, that is transformative.

The 15% figure is not theoretical. It represents documented results from fleets that have systematically implemented TMS-driven fuel management programs. The savings come from four distinct levers: smarter route planning that reduces miles driven, fuel price optimization that reduces cost per gallon, driver behavior management that improves miles per gallon, and idle reduction that eliminates fuel consumption that produces no freight revenue.

None of these levers require expensive technology beyond a competently configured TMS. What they do require is commitment to using the data the TMS generates, acting on what it reveals, and holding drivers and dispatchers accountable to the results.

## Lever 1: Route Optimization and Deadhead Reduction

The most direct fuel savings come from driving fewer miles. A TMS with route optimization identifies the shortest or most fuel-efficient routing for each load, accounts for real-time traffic and road conditions, and minimizes deadhead miles between loads.

**Deadhead Reduction**

Empty miles — trucks running without revenue freight — are the most expensive fuel consumption in any fleet. A truck running 200 deadhead miles per week at 6.5 MPG burns 31 gallons of diesel generating zero revenue. At $3.90 per gallon, that is $120 per week per truck, or $6,240 annually. For a 20-truck fleet, total deadhead costs can exceed $100,000 per year.

A TMS with load matching functionality reduces deadhead by connecting outbound loads with return freight opportunities. Even modest improvements — reducing deadhead from 20% of miles to 15% — translate directly to fuel savings at no degradation in service quality.

**Case Study: Regional Carrier, Ontario**

A 15-truck regional carrier based in Brampton, Ontario implemented TMS route optimization in Q3 2024. Prior to TMS adoption, dispatchers planned routes manually using Google Maps and carrier relationships built over years. Average deadhead was 22% of total miles.

After six months on TMS, deadhead dropped to 14% of total miles — an 8-percentage-point improvement driven by better load matching and multi-stop sequencing. Fuel savings from the deadhead reduction alone were approximately $87,000 annually on a fleet of 15 units. Combined with the fuel price optimization described below, total first-year fuel savings exceeded $130,000.

**Multi-Stop Sequencing**

For carriers running multi-stop routes — LTL operations, delivery services, food distribution — the sequence in which stops are serviced affects total mileage significantly. A TMS with vehicle routing optimization solves multi-stop sequencing as a traveling salesman problem, minimizing total distance. Manual dispatch sequencing typically produces routes 10–20% longer than the optimized solution. On a 50-stop-per-day delivery fleet, that is a material fuel difference.

## Lever 2: Fuel Price Optimization

Buying diesel at the right price is a separate savings lever from driving fewer miles. A TMS that integrates fuel price data enables fleet managers to direct drivers to the lowest-cost fueling point on their route, not simply the most convenient.

**Price Data Integration**

Modern TMS platforms can integrate with OPIS fuel price feeds, fuel card network pricing APIs, and loyalty program data to show dispatchers and drivers the current price at stops along each route. When a driver has a 200-gallon fill opportunity and two stops are within acceptable routing distance, the TMS can calculate the total cost difference — accounting for any deviation miles — and direct the driver to the better buy.

**Fuel Card Network Management**

Most carriers run one or more commercial fuel cards — EFS, Comdata, WEX, or similar — that provide negotiated network pricing at partner locations. A TMS that integrates with the carrier's fuel card system can track actual cost-per-gallon by driver, by lane, and by stop, identifying systematic overpayment patterns.

Common fuel overpayment patterns revealed by TMS fuel card data:
- Drivers consistently fueling at stops outside the negotiated network, paying rack pricing instead of network pricing
- Fuel purchases in high-tax jurisdictions that could have been tankered from lower-tax states purchased earlier in the run
- Fuel stops at isolated locations with no competition, paying the isolation premium

Identifying these patterns through TMS data and correcting them through driver coaching and dispatch guidance typically delivers 3–6 cents per gallon improvement across the fleet.

**Tankering Optimization**

A TMS with real-time price data can calculate the optimal fuel load at each stop based on upcoming price differentials, tank capacity, and load weight. This tankering optimization — ensuring drivers carry maximum fuel through expensive jurisdictions rather than buying locally — is one of the cleaner fleet-level fuel cost levers. On routes that cross California or the Northeast, tankering from adjacent lower-tax states can save 20–40 cents per gallon on every gallon that does not get purchased in the expensive jurisdiction.

## Lever 3: Driver Behavior Monitoring

After route and price optimization, the next largest fuel savings opportunity is driver behavior. Fuel consumption for identical trucks on identical routes can vary by 15–20% based on individual driver habits — specifically speed, braking patterns, idle time, and transmission management.

**Speed Management**

Aerodynamic drag increases with the square of velocity. A truck running at 68 MPH versus 62 MPH uses approximately 15% more fuel on that segment. For a driver running 600 miles per day, a consistent 6-MPH speed reduction — fully within HOS and legal limits on most corridors — represents a significant fuel efficiency gain.

TMS telemetry, typically fed by ELD data, produces per-driver speed reports showing average speeds by segment, percentage of time above a defined threshold, and comparison against fleet average. Fleets that implement a speed coaching program based on TMS data consistently report 4–8% MPG improvement among the drivers who shift their habits.

**Harsh Braking and Acceleration**

Harsh braking events are expensive twice: once in diesel burned during the acceleration that preceded the brake event, and once in brake wear. A truck that accelerates hard from a traffic light and then brakes hard at the next one burns fuel and wears consumables that a driver using progressive throttle management would not.

TMS platforms that receive ATIS (Advanced Driver Assistance Systems) data from modern ELDs can score each driver on harsh event frequency — aggressive braking, rapid acceleration, sharp cornering — and produce coaching reports. Drivers who score highest on aggressive event frequency consistently show the worst fuel economy in fleet analysis.

**Progressive Idle Monitoring**

Engine idling burns approximately 0.8 gallons per hour. A driver who idles 6 hours per day during overnight rest burns 4.8 gallons — $18.72 at $3.90/gallon — producing no freight movement. Across a 20-truck fleet, that is $374 per day or $136,000 per year in idle fuel.

TMS idle reports break down idle time by driver and by period (overnight, in-traffic, loading dock wait). Armed with this data, fleet managers can identify the drivers with the highest idle costs, investigate whether the idle is operational (dock waiting) or avoidable (overnight cab heating that an APU would handle more efficiently), and take targeted action.

## Lever 4: Preventive Maintenance Timing

A less obvious TMS fuel savings lever is maintenance scheduling. An engine that is overdue for an oil change, running on worn air filters, or operating with a slow tire pressure leak runs less efficiently than a properly maintained unit. A TMS that tracks odometer-based maintenance intervals and flags overdue items ensures that deferred maintenance does not silently erode fuel economy fleet-wide.

**Tire Pressure Management**

A tire underinflated by 10 PSI increases rolling resistance and reduces fuel economy by approximately 0.5%. On a typical 18-wheel Class 8 truck with 18 tires, even modest underinflation across multiple tires — a common condition in fleets without rigorous pre-trip inspection enforcement — can cost 1–3% in fuel efficiency.

TMS platforms that integrate with TPMS (Tire Pressure Monitoring Systems) via ELD data streams can alert fleet managers to vehicles with chronic tire pressure issues. Targeted maintenance intervention — checking, inflating, and inspecting the tires on those specific trucks — costs an hour of shop time and recovers weeks of lost fuel efficiency.

**Air Filter and DPF Monitoring**

Clogged air filters and diesel particulate filters both increase fuel consumption. A DPF that needs regeneration more frequently than it should — because it is accumulating soot faster than normal — is a symptom of a maintenance or operational issue that a TMS maintenance module can track if equipped with the relevant telematics.

## Putting the Levers Together: A 15% Roadmap

A 20-truck carrier that implements all four levers systematically can realistically achieve the following annual fuel savings:

| Lever | Typical Annual Savings |
|---|---|
| Route optimization / deadhead reduction | $80,000–$120,000 |
| Fuel price optimization (price data + tankering) | $40,000–$70,000 |
| Driver behavior coaching (speed + idle) | $50,000–$90,000 |
| Maintenance optimization (tire pressure, air filters) | $15,000–$30,000 |
| **Total** | **$185,000–$310,000** |

Against a baseline of $1.44 million in annual fuel, the midpoint of that range — $248,000 — is approximately 17%.

The caveat is implementation. Each lever requires both a TMS configured to capture and surface the relevant data, and a fleet manager who acts on what the data reveals. A TMS sitting in the corner generating reports that no one reviews delivers none of these savings. The technology enables the savings; the management process realizes them.

Fleets that have achieved the documented 15%+ results share a common operational posture: they treat fuel cost as a daily operational metric, not a monthly accounting line. They review driver fuel efficiency weekly, adjust routing based on current price data, and hold a consistent position on speed governance. The TMS is the instrument; the manager is the player.
