## Cross-Border Trucking and the Importance of the Last Stop Before the Line

The US-Canada border handles approximately $800 billion in bilateral trade annually, and commercial trucks carry the majority of that freight by value. There are roughly 120 commercial land crossings along the nearly 9,000-kilometre border, but a handful of them account for the overwhelming share of truck traffic — and the stops immediately before each of those crossings have evolved into critical preparation zones for drivers.

The truck stop near a major border crossing is not just a place to fuel and sleep. It is where drivers verify their paperwork, check their load against the manifest, confirm their eManifest ACE or ACI filing, take their mandatory 30-minute break, and stage for an early-morning crossing when lines are shortest. Getting this last stop right can mean the difference between a smooth two-hour crossing at 6:00 AM and a six-hour delay at noon.

This guide covers the four highest-volume US-Canada commercial crossings and the best stops near each one, along with practical guidance on how to use those stops most effectively.

## Detroit/Windsor: The Ambassador Bridge and the DRIC Corridor

The Ambassador Bridge between Windsor, Ontario, and Detroit, Michigan, is the single busiest international border crossing in North America by commercial vehicle volume, handling approximately 8,000 trucks per day. The nearby Windsor-Detroit Tunnel handles passenger traffic but no commercial trucks. The Gordie Howe International Bridge, under construction and targeted for completion in late 2024/2025, will add significant capacity to this corridor.

**US Side — Detroit Area**

On the US side, the I-75 corridor through Detroit and the I-94 interchange area have multiple fuel and service options:

- **Pilot Flying J at Woodhaven, MI (I-75 exit 32):** A large full-service location approximately 20 miles south of the Ambassador Bridge, well-positioned for southbound pre-crossing staging or northbound post-crossing fuel. Good parking, reliable showers, 24-hour restaurant.
- **Love's Travel Stop at Monroe, MI (I-75):** Another solid option farther south, better for drivers staging the night before a morning crossing and not wanting to navigate Detroit-area congestion.
- **TA Truckstop at Taylor, MI (I-75 & Eureka Road):** Closer to the Ambassador Bridge approach, good for last-stop fuel before crossing.

**Canadian Side — Windsor Area**

Windsor has a denser commercial services area than many border cities:

- **Petro-Canada on Huron Church Road:** Huron Church Road (Highway 3) is the designated truck route to the Ambassador Bridge from the Windsor side. Multiple fuel options exist on this corridor, with Petro-Canada being the most reliable for commercial card acceptance.
- **Husky at Highway 401/Windsor area:** A well-positioned stop on the 401 west of Windsor for drivers coming from the east who need a last fuel before the crossing.
- **Windsor Truck Stop (independent, Ojibway Parkway area):** A dedicated truck stop serving the port-of-entry area with parking specifically designed for pre-border staging.

**Customs Prep Tips for Ambassador Bridge**

The Ambassador Bridge's primary inspection plaza handles both CBSA (Canada Border Services Agency) and CBP (US Customs and Border Protection) in separate plazas. For northbound (US to Canada) crossings:

- File your ACI eManifest at least one hour before arrival at the border — CBSA requires 1-hour advance notice for highway carriers.
- Have your PARS/PAPS barcode printed and visible, or confirm your broker has transmitted it.
- Stage for a 5:00–6:00 AM crossing when commercial lane wait times are shortest. The stop at Taylor or Woodhaven allows you to time your departure for this window.

## Buffalo/Niagara: Peace Bridge and Lewiston-Queenston

The Peace Bridge at Buffalo/Fort Erie and the Lewiston-Queenston Bridge handle substantial commercial traffic serving the southern Ontario manufacturing corridor and the US Northeast. Both crossings are active commercial ports, though the Peace Bridge handles higher commercial volumes.

**US Side — Buffalo Area**

- **Pilot Flying J at Dunkirk, NY (I-90 exit 59):** Dunkirk is approximately 50 miles southwest of the Peace Bridge — a practical overnight staging stop for drivers approaching from the west or southwest.
- **Love's Travel Stop at Angola, NY (I-90 exit 57A):** Similar positioning to Dunkirk, with good overnight parking and an easy morning approach to Buffalo.
- **TA Truckstop at Buffalo (I-90/I-190 interchange area):** Closer to the crossing, but in a denser urban environment. Better for last-minute fuel than overnight staging.
- **Lewiston area (near Lewiston-Queenston Bridge):** Smaller independent fuel stops near the Lewiston approach. The Lewiston bridge is often less congested than Peace Bridge and worth considering for southbound crossings.

**Canadian Side — Fort Erie and Niagara Falls**

- **Petro-Canada at Fort Erie (QEW/Ridge Road area):** The natural first Canadian stop after crossing the Peace Bridge, with truck fueling and basic amenities.
- **Husky at Niagara Falls (Highway 405/QEW corridor):** A larger facility serving the Niagara corridor, useful for northbound drivers staging before the Queenston-Lewiston crossing.
- **Independent yards in Niagara Falls industrial zone (Thorold Stone Road area):** Private parking options for drivers making early-morning crossings.

**Crossing Strategy for Niagara**

The Peace Bridge operates 24 hours and has dedicated FAST lane infrastructure for approved carriers. If your carrier is enrolled in the Customs-Trade Partnership Against Terrorism (C-TPAT) on the US side or Partners in Protection (PIP) on the Canadian side, confirm your crossing is using the FAST lane — it will typically cut your wait time in half. The Lewiston-Queenston Bridge is worth monitoring as an alternative when Peace Bridge commercial lanes are backed up; it is 15 minutes farther by road but frequently less congested.

## Blaine/Surrey: Pacific Highway and Peace Arch Crossings

The Pacific Highway crossing between Blaine, Washington, and Surrey, British Columbia, is the primary commercial crossing on the West Coast. The Peace Arch crossing adjacent to it handles passenger traffic. Together, this corridor serves the significant container-truck traffic moving between Port of Vancouver and the US Pacific Northwest and beyond.

**US Side — Bellingham and Blaine Area**

- **Love's Travel Stop at Burlington, WA (I-5 exit 229):** Approximately 30 miles south of the border, Burlington is the major truck services hub for this corridor. Large parking lot, full amenities, and easy I-5 access.
- **Pilot Flying J at Burlington, WA:** A competing option at the same exit cluster, with slightly different amenities. Both Love's and Pilot in Burlington are heavily used by US-Canada freight drivers.
- **Flying J at Ferndale, WA (I-5 exit 262):** Closer to the border, approximately 10 miles south of Blaine. Practical for last-stop fuel before crossing.
- **Pilot at Blaine, WA:** The closest major stop to the crossing, approximately 1–2 miles from the inspection plaza. Useful for final paperwork review stops but limited parking.

**Canadian Side — Surrey and Delta**

The BC side of this crossing has good infrastructure in the surrounding industrial zones:

- **Husky at Surrey (64th Avenue / King George Boulevard):** A key commercial stop serving the South Surrey/White Rock industrial corridor, approximately 10 minutes from the Pacific Highway crossing.
- **Petro-Canada at Cloverdale (Highway 10 area):** A reliable stop for eastbound BC drivers approaching from the Fraser Valley.
- **Private yards in Surrey's 64th Avenue industrial zone:** Multiple independent secured lots serve Pacific Highway crossing traffic. These fill quickly during port peak periods — advance booking through a parking platform is strongly recommended.

**West Coast Crossing Timing**

The Pacific Highway crossing experiences significant morning peaks Monday–Friday as port and perishable freight rushes across. The 3:00 AM–6:00 AM window is typically shortest wait. Stage at the Ferndale or Burlington location to time your approach for this window. For northbound crossings into BC, the CBSA's Border Wait Time portal (accessible through the CBSA website and BorderXing app) gives real-time commercial lane wait estimates.

## Champlain/Lacolle: The Quebec-New York Corridor

The Champlain crossing between Lacolle, Quebec, and Champlain, New York, is the primary commercial crossing on the Quebec-New York corridor, handling freight between Montreal and New York City, New England, and the US Northeast.

**US Side — Plattsburgh and Champlain Area**

- **Pilot Flying J at Plattsburgh, NY (I-87 exit 35):** Plattsburgh is approximately 25 miles south of the border and the primary US staging area for this crossing. Good amenities, reliable parking, and easy I-87 access make this the standard overnight stop for drivers approaching the Champlain crossing from the south.
- **Love's Travel Stop at Plattsburgh, NY:** An alternative at a different exit on I-87, with comparable facilities.
- **Independent stop near Champlain, NY (US 9):** Smaller fuel options in the immediate border zone exist on US Route 9 approaching the crossing.

**Canadian Side — Lacolle and South Shore Montreal**

The Quebec side of this crossing is predominantly rural industrial:

- **Petro-Canada at Lacolle (Highway 15/A-15):** The first major fuel option after crossing into Quebec, on the A-15/Autoroute des Laurentides heading north toward Montreal.
- **Brossard/Saint-Hubert (Highway 30/A-30 corridor):** For drivers continuing into Montreal or the South Shore, the Autoroute 30 commercial corridor has fuel and private parking options in the Brossard and Saint-Hubert industrial zones.
- **Longueuil industrial yards:** For Montreal deliveries, Longueuil private truck yards are the practical overnight stop (see the Quebec section in the Canada truck stops guide).

**Champlain Crossing Notes**

The Champlain crossing is smaller than the Windsor or Niagara crossings and can experience significant wait times during summer tourist periods when it shares infrastructure with passenger traffic. Commercial lanes are separate but can back up during passenger peak hours. The CBSA eManifest one-hour advance filing requirement applies here as at all commercial crossings.

## General Cross-Border Stop Best Practices

**File eManifest Early**

For northbound crossings into Canada, your carrier must file an ACI (Advance Commercial Information) eManifest at least one hour before arrival. Many experienced drivers file at the last US truck stop — Pilot, Love's, or whatever facility is your staging point — to confirm the filing is complete before they approach the border. Never assume your broker or dispatcher has handled this; confirm it yourself.

For southbound crossings into the US, your ACE eManifest must be filed by the carrier. The requirement is similar: file before you depart your last Canadian stop, and confirm it in your CBP system.

**Use the Last Stop for Documentation Review**

The truck stop before the border is the right place to:

- Verify your bill of lading matches your manifest
- Confirm your PARS or PAPS number is printed and readable
- Check that your driver's licence, medical card, and carrier operating authority documents are current and accessible
- For hazmat loads, confirm placard placement and emergency response documentation

**Stage for Morning Crossings**

The lowest commercial lane wait times at every major crossing are consistently in the 3:00 AM–7:00 AM window. Stage at a facility that allows you to time your arrival at the crossing for 5:00–6:00 AM. An overnight stay at the last US or Canadian stop — depending on your crossing direction — is almost always faster overall than approaching at noon on a weekday.

**Pre-Clear in FAST/NEXUS When Possible**

If your carrier is enrolled in C-TPAT (US) and PIP (Canada), and your load is from an approved supplier, you qualify for FAST lane crossing. The time savings are significant: average FAST lane times at major commercial crossings are typically 15–30 minutes versus 60–180 minutes in standard lanes during peak periods.

Cross-border freight is the backbone of North American trade, and the stops near the major crossings are a critical support infrastructure for that trade. Using them strategically — for staging, documentation review, mandatory rest, and optimal crossing timing — is a fundamental skill for any driver working the US-Canada corridor.
