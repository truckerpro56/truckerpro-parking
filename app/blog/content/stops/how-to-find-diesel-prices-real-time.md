## Why Real-Time Diesel Price Data Matters More Than Ever

Diesel prices at US truck stops can vary by more than 40 cents per gallon between locations on the same corridor on the same day. That is not an edge case — it is a routine reality created by the interaction of state fuel taxes, proximity to refineries, local competition, and individual stop pricing strategies. For an owner-operator filling 200 gallons, a 40-cent-per-gallon swing is an $80 difference on a single fuel stop. Over a 120,000-mile year with roughly two fill-ups per week, systematic price intelligence is worth thousands of dollars annually.

The challenge is that diesel price data has a short shelf life. Prices change multiple times per week at most stops — sometimes multiple times per day during periods of crude oil volatility. A price you checked yesterday morning may not reflect today's rack. This is why real-time or near-real-time data sources are essential, and why understanding which tools give you accurate, current data versus which ones lag behind by hours or days determines whether the information is actually actionable.

## Driver-Contributed Price Networks: The Most Current Data

The most accurate source of current diesel prices at specific truck stops is other drivers who fueled there recently. Driver-contributed data networks aggregate these reports and publish them in map-based apps that let you see prices at upcoming stops before you arrive.

**Trucker Path**

Trucker Path is purpose-built for commercial drivers and is the most widely used truck-specific app for fuel price tracking. Drivers submit prices directly from the app immediately after fueling — the timestamp on each report tells you exactly how recent it is. The app covers thousands of US and Canadian locations with commercial truck fuel.

Trucker Path's price data tends to be the most current available because the contributor base is large and active. On busy corridors like I-40, I-80, and I-35, you will typically find prices reported within the last two to four hours at major stops. On rural or lower-traffic corridors, data can be older — always check the timestamp.

Beyond fuel prices, Trucker Path shows parking availability, CAT Scale locations, weigh station alerts, weather conditions, and driver reviews. For a driver who uses only one app, it is the most comprehensive option.

**GasBuddy (with Truck Map Mode)**

GasBuddy's main product is consumer gas prices, but the platform has a truck-focused mode that filters to diesel prices at commercial stops. GasBuddy's contributor network is larger in absolute terms than Trucker Path's, but the contributor base is more mixed — many reporters are passenger vehicle drivers who do not frequent truck-specific fueling lanes.

For major truck stops on high-traffic corridors, GasBuddy data is generally reliable and frequently updated. For smaller independent stops, coverage can be spotty and data can lag by a day or more. GasBuddy's real advantage is breadth: if you need a price estimate at a smaller independent stop where Trucker Path has no recent reports, GasBuddy may have one.

**Waze (for spot-check context only)**

Waze collects fuel prices at gas stations, including some truck stop locations, but its commercial truck coverage is unreliable. Use Waze data for context only, not for planning commercial fuel stops.

## Chain Apps: Real-Time Pricing at Specific Networks

The major truck stop chains publish current diesel prices for their own locations through their loyalty apps. This data is reliable and updated frequently — in many cases in near-real-time as the chain adjusts its pricing.

**Pilot myRewards Plus App**

The Pilot app shows current diesel prices at all Pilot and Flying J locations nationwide. Critically, it shows two prices: the posted retail price and the myRewards price (what you pay as a loyalty member). The gap between these two figures is typically 2–5 cents per gallon, which is meaningful at scale.

The app also shows your upcoming Pilot locations on the route, lets you filter by amenities, and shows estimated shower wait times at equipped locations. For drivers who run Pilot/Flying J as their primary network, this is the first app to open when planning a fuel stop.

**Love's My Love Rewards App**

Love's app similarly shows current diesel prices at Love's locations and your enrolled loyalty pricing. The Love's app also shows real-time shower availability — a unique feature that lets you see whether showers are available before you commit to a stop. For drivers who earn free showers through My Love Rewards and want to plan their shower stop around availability, this data is genuinely useful.

**TA/Petro UltraONE App**

The UltraONE app shows TA and Petro prices. TA/Petro pricing tends to be less variable than Pilot or Love's because more of their volume runs through fleet card contracts, but the app still shows current retail and enrolled pricing for planning purposes.

## Fuel Card Platforms: The Most Accurate Pricing for Card Holders

If you operate under a fleet fuel card — EFS, Comdata, WEX, Relay, or OTR — your card's pricing platform provides the most accurate diesel price data available to you, because it shows the actual rate you will pay with your card, not the posted retail price.

**EFS (Electronic Funds Source) Network Pricing**

EFS cardholders can access network pricing through the EFS app and portal. The prices shown reflect your actual card agreement rate at each participating stop — far more useful than a driver-contributed estimate that reflects the retail pump price someone else paid.

**Comdata Fuel Locator**

Comdata's platform, accessible via the Comdata app and web portal, shows network pricing at Comdata-accepting locations. For carriers and owner-operators using Comdata, this tool should be the primary price-checking resource because the prices shown are what you will actually pay.

**Relay and OTR Fuel Card Apps**

Both Relay and OTR publish their per-gallon savings versus posted retail through their apps. These are particularly useful for independent owner-operators who do not have access to a carrier's negotiated Comdata or EFS rate — the app shows you, by location, what you will save versus what a non-card driver pays.

## TMS Integration: Systematic Fuel Price Tracking

Individual app checks tell you the price at one stop, at one moment. A well-configured TMS (Transportation Management System) does something more valuable: it tracks your actual per-gallon cost over time, across all your stops, and surfaces the patterns that individual price checks cannot reveal.

**What TMS fuel analytics shows you:**

- Your average cost-per-gallon on each lane you run regularly, so you can compare your performance against corridor benchmarks
- Which stops in your regular network consistently price above or below the corridor average, letting you prioritize the better-priced stops over time
- Your actual miles-per-gallon by season, load type, and route, showing you whether fuel efficiency is improving or degrading
- Fuel spend as a percentage of gross revenue, the key metric for monitoring whether fuel costs are in line with your business economics

The distinction between checking a price app before a single stop versus reviewing TMS analytics at the end of a week is the difference between optimizing individual transactions and optimizing a fuel cost system. Experienced owner-operators and fleet managers do both.

## Practical Workflow: How to Check Prices Before a Stop

A workable real-time diesel price workflow looks like this:

**1. Check your fuel card app first.** If you have an EFS, Comdata, or Relay card, check your card's app first to see the price you will actually pay at upcoming stops. This is your baseline.

**2. Cross-reference with Trucker Path.** Open Trucker Path and look at driver-reported prices at the same stops. If there is a significant gap between Trucker Path's reported retail price and your card price, confirm the card pricing is current in your card's app.

**3. Check the chain app for your primary network.** If you are Pilot-primary or Love's-primary, open that chain's app to see loyalty pricing and confirm it is lower than Trucker Path's reported retail.

**4. Look 60–90 miles ahead, not just your next stop.** If stop B is 15 cents cheaper per gallon than stop A but you can make it there on fuel, the math usually favors skipping stop A. Trucker Path's map view makes this ahead-planning visible.

**5. Note the data timestamp.** A Trucker Path report from 6 hours ago may not reflect current pricing. On volatile crude days, prices can shift multiple times. If a report is more than 4 hours old and the price seems unusually low, verify with the chain app before planning around it.

## Building Longer-Term Price Intelligence

Beyond the tools above, experienced drivers build personal fuel price intelligence over time:

**Know your regular lanes cold.** If you run I-40 Albuquerque to Amarillo regularly, you will learn which stops are consistently priced well and which are routinely above corridor average. This knowledge does not expire on a single day's price movement — it builds into a reliable mental map that reduces how much live checking you need to do.

**Track fuel tax state transitions.** California, Connecticut, Indiana, and New York carry among the highest total diesel taxes in the country. Wyoming, New Mexico, and Tennessee are among the lowest. Knowing these transitions lets you anticipate where prices will structurally jump, independent of market conditions.

**Use your TMS fuel history.** Review your actual per-gallon averages by corridor quarterly. If one lane is consistently expensive and there are routing alternatives, this data makes the business case for switching.

Real-time diesel price tools are genuinely useful — they remove the guesswork from individual stop decisions. But they work best as part of a broader fuel cost system built on lane knowledge, card pricing, and TMS analytics.
