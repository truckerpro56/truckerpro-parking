## Why Truck Parking Regulations Matter More Than You Think

Parking a commercial vehicle is never as simple as pulling over and shutting off the engine. In Canada, truck parking is governed by an overlapping web of federal Hours of Service regulations, provincial highway traffic acts, and municipal bylaws — and what is legal in one jurisdiction can result in a substantial fine 50 kilometres down the road.

Transport Canada's Hours of Service regulations require drivers to take mandatory rest periods, but they do not guarantee that a legal place to rest will be available when you need one. That gap between the legal requirement to stop and the practical availability of places to stop is at the heart of Canada's truck parking problem. Understanding the rules — and the consequences of breaking them — is essential knowledge for every professional driver and fleet manager operating in Canada.

This guide provides a province-by-province breakdown of the regulations that matter most to commercial drivers.

## Federal Framework: Hours of Service and the Parking Obligation

Before diving into provincial rules, it is important to understand the federal baseline. Transport Canada's Hours of Service Regulations (SOR/2005-313, as amended) require commercial drivers to:

- Take a minimum 8-consecutive-hour off-duty period within any 24-hour window
- Not exceed 13 hours of driving time after 8 hours off
- Not exceed 14 hours of on-duty time after 8 hours off
- Comply with the 70-hour/7-day or 120-hour/14-day cycle limits

These requirements create a mandatory obligation to stop driving — but they carry no provision about where that stop must happen. A driver whose HOS clock expires at a location with no legal truck parking is still legally required to stop. Parking on a highway shoulder, on a residential street, or in a fire route does not become legal simply because the driver had no alternative. This is why proactive parking planning is a compliance matter, not just a convenience matter.

Drivers using electronic logging devices (ELDs) — which have been mandatory for federally regulated inter-provincial carriers since January 2023 — have their location and duty status automatically recorded. An HOS violation that previously might have been resolved informally now creates a timestamped, location-tagged record.

## Ontario

Ontario has the most detailed and aggressively enforced truck parking rules in Canada, reflecting the province's position as the hub of Canadian freight movement.

**Highway Traffic Act Restrictions**

Under the Ontario Highway Traffic Act, parking on provincial highways is prohibited except in designated areas. This applies to all 400-series highways and most numbered provincial routes. The prohibition includes stopping for reasons other than mechanical failure, medical emergency, or enforcement direction — drivers who stop on 400-series highway shoulders for non-emergency rest risk fines under Section 170 of the HTA.

**Municipal Bylaws — GTA**

Every municipality in the Greater Toronto Area has its own approach to truck parking, and the rules are not consistent across municipal borders. In general:

- **Toronto:** Overnight truck parking prohibited on streets city-wide. Enforcement through bylaw officers is active, particularly in North York industrial areas where residents have complained about overnight truck traffic.
- **Brampton:** Truck parking prohibited on residential streets at all times. Commercial/industrial zones permit daytime parking but many have 3-hour limits for vehicles not actively loading/unloading.
- **Mississauga:** Similar to Brampton. Several industrial areas near the airport have been subject to crackdowns specifically targeting overnight truck parking.
- **Vaughan:** Broader industrial zones with more parking tolerance, but the Highway 400 corridor areas are heavily monitored.

Fines for parking violations under GTA municipal bylaws range from $200 to $500 for commercial vehicles, with repeat violations potentially resulting in vehicle towing at owner expense.

**Ministry of Transportation Rest Areas**

Ontario's MTO operates designated rest areas along major corridors. These are the safest and most straightforward places to stop legally, but they do not accept reservations and operate on a first-come basis. During peak periods, the rest areas at Milton (Highway 401), Port Hope (Highway 115), and Barrie (Highway 400) regularly fill before 10:00 PM.

**Weigh Station Parking**

Ontario's Commercial Vehicle Inspection Stations (at locations like Tilbury, Woodstock, and Hagersville) do not permit overnight parking except in specifically designated areas. Drivers who park in a weigh station area outside designated spots face enforcement action.

## Quebec

Quebec's truck parking regulations are administered primarily through the Ministère des Transports du Québec (MTQ) and municipal authorities. A key practical consideration: regulatory signage in Quebec is in French only, and drivers unfamiliar with French-language road signs may inadvertently violate parking restrictions.

**Autoroute System**

Quebec prohibits stopping on autoroutes (the A-series controlled-access highways) except at designated aires de repos (rest areas) or in genuine emergencies. The A-20 between Montreal and Quebec City and the A-40 in the Montreal metropolitan area are heavily patrolled by Sûreté du Québec.

**Montreal and Area**

The City of Montreal prohibits commercial vehicle parking on streets in the urban core at all times and in residential zones overnight (typically 11 PM to 7 AM). Arrondissement (borough) rules vary somewhat, but the default is restrictive. The Rosemont, Villeray, and Mercier boroughs have particularly active enforcement.

**Weight Restrictions and Seasonal Load Limits**

Quebec's spring load restriction period (Dégel, typically March through May) is the most aggressive in Canada. During dégel, many secondary roads have dramatically reduced weight limits. Trucks parked on restricted roads during dégel are not exempt from these restrictions — being stationary doesn't eliminate the road damage caused by a heavy vehicle's static load. Fines for dégel violations can reach $5,000 to $30,000 for seriously overweight vehicles.

## British Columbia

BC's commercial vehicle regulations are administered by the Commercial Vehicle Safety and Enforcement (CVSE) division of the Ministry of Transportation.

**Highway Parking**

BC Regulation 26/58 under the Motor Vehicle Act prohibits parking on provincial highways except in designated areas. Enforcement on the Trans-Canada (Highway 1), the Coquihalla (Highway 5), and the Sea-to-Sky Highway (Highway 99) is strict.

**Lower Mainland**

Metro Vancouver municipalities have inconsistent truck parking rules. Surrey's industrial zones are the most parking-friendly, while Vancouver proper, Burnaby, and Richmond have strict prohibitions. The City of Richmond, which serves YVR and the port approaches, has designated certain industrial areas as overnight truck-friendly, but access to those zones from the highway network requires navigating residential areas where trucks are prohibited.

**Mountain and Rural Corridors**

BC's mountain passes present particular challenges. The Coquihalla rest areas have limited capacity, and the designated chain-up areas are not overnight parking zones. Drivers running the Trans-Canada through Revelstoke or Rogers Pass should plan their overnight stops at Kamloops, Salmon Arm, or Golden, where private facilities exist.

## Alberta

Alberta Transportation regulates commercial vehicle parking under the Traffic Safety Act and its regulations. Alberta is generally more parking-friendly than BC or Ontario, but urban centres have their own rules.

**Calgary and Edmonton**

Both cities have active commercial vehicle enforcement programs. Residential street parking for commercial vehicles is prohibited overnight in most zones. Industrial areas are generally available, and both cities have multiple private truck parking facilities that accept walk-in and advance bookings.

**Seasonal Road Restrictions**

Alberta has a spring weight restriction program similar to Quebec's, affecting secondary highways typically from March through May. The restrictions vary by road class and current conditions. Overweight violations in Alberta carry fines of $5 to $10 per kilogram over the limit, which can accumulate quickly for seriously overweight loads.

## Saskatchewan

Saskatchewan Highway Traffic Act regulations apply to all provincial highways. The province has fewer formal rest areas than Ontario or Quebec, but parking enforcement along rural highways is less intensive.

The municipalities of Regina and Saskatoon have commercial vehicle parking bylaws similar to other Prairie cities — industrial zones are generally accessible, and enforcement of overnight parking in residential areas is active. Fines typically range from $150 to $350.

## Manitoba

Manitoba's Highway Traffic Act and Highways Protection Act govern commercial vehicle parking. Winnipeg has dedicated commercial vehicle enforcement officers and prohibits overnight truck parking in residential zones city-wide. The industrial areas of north Winnipeg (Inkster, Panet Road, Logan Avenue) are the standard alternative and have several purpose-built facilities.

Rural Manitoba highway rest areas are available but limited in number and without the amenities of Ontario or Quebec facilities.

## Nova Scotia

Nova Scotia regulates commercial vehicles under the Motor Vehicle Act. The province has fewer commercial vehicle-specific parking provisions than larger provinces, reflecting its lower freight volumes. The Highway 104 corridor and the Trans-Canada through the Cobequid Pass have adequate rest area coverage for current traffic levels.

Halifax has commercial vehicle parking restrictions in the downtown and residential areas, with enforcement increasing in recent years due to port-related freight growth. Dartmouth industrial areas provide the most practical alternatives.

## New Brunswick

New Brunswick's Motor Vehicle Act applies standard commercial vehicle parking restrictions on provincial highways. The province serves as the primary land corridor between Quebec and Nova Scotia/PEI, and the Trans-Canada (Highway 1) through Moncton and Fredericton has adequate parking infrastructure for current volumes.

The Irving Oil network of truck stops is particularly well-developed in New Brunswick and serves as a de facto parking infrastructure along the TransCanada corridor.

## Understanding Fines: What You Actually Risk

Parking violations for commercial vehicles in Canada carry fines at three levels:

1. **Municipal bylaw violations** — $150 to $500, depending on the city and specific infraction. These do not typically generate CVOR (Commercial Vehicle Operator's Registration) points in Ontario, but repeat violations can trigger enhanced enforcement attention.

2. **Provincial highway act violations** — $200 to $1,000 for improper stopping or parking on provincial highways. These may generate CVOR points in Ontario and equivalent demerit points in other provinces.

3. **Weight and dimension violations** (for overweight/oversize parking) — $500 to $30,000+, calculated per kilogram over limit in most provinces. These are among the most financially significant penalties in commercial vehicle law.

Fleet operators should ensure drivers understand that a $300 parking ticket is not simply the cost of convenience — it is also a data point in enforcement databases that can trigger roadside inspection patterns and CVOR review thresholds.
