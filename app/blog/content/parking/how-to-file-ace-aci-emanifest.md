## eManifest: The Backbone of Cross-Border Compliance

Electronic manifest filing — eManifest — is the pre-arrival notification system that tells border agencies what is coming before it arrives. Both Canada and the United States operate mandatory electronic manifest programs for commercial highway carriers. Failure to file correctly and on time is not a minor administrative issue: penalties begin in the thousands of dollars per violation and can ultimately result in a carrier being barred from crossing the border.

Despite the stakes, eManifest errors are common. Experienced dispatchers make them. Experienced drivers make them. The reasons are usually the same: confusion between ACI and ACE requirements, unclear data on the bill of lading, last-minute load changes without manifest updates, or unfamiliarity with the filing window requirements. This guide addresses all of those failure points.

## ACI vs ACE: Two Systems, Two Directions

The most important foundational concept in cross-border manifest filing is understanding that ACI and ACE are separate, independent systems operated by different governments.

**ACI (Advance Commercial Information)** is Canada's system, operated by the Canada Border Services Agency (CBSA). You file an ACI eManifest when you are importing goods **into Canada** — meaning when you are crossing the border northbound with a loaded trailer.

**ACE (Automated Commercial Environment)** is the US system, operated by US Customs and Border Protection (CBP). You file an ACE manifest when you are importing goods **into the United States** — meaning when you are crossing the border southbound with a loaded trailer.

Empty trailer crossings are generally exempt from manifest requirements in both systems, but confirm with your customs broker on any load where empty status could be disputed (e.g., partial load returns, cargo residue in tanks, trailer accessory equipment).

If you run a round-trip — loaded into the US, returning loaded to Canada — you will file an ACE manifest for the southbound crossing and an ACI manifest for the northbound crossing. These are separate filings for separate border events and must be managed independently.

## ACI eManifest: Filing for Entry Into Canada

### What Must Be Filed

CBSA's ACI program requires three types of pre-arrival data, each filed by a different party.

**Carrier pre-arrival (your responsibility as the carrier):**
- Carrier code (your CBSA-assigned carrier number)
- Conveyance reference number
- Expected port of entry and estimated arrival time
- Truck plate and jurisdiction
- Trailer plate and jurisdiction (if applicable)
- Driver information: full legal name, date of birth, citizenship, driver's licence number and province/state of issue

**Freight forwarder or customs broker data:**
- House bill of lading or equivalent document reference
- Shipper name and address
- Consignee name and address

**Importer or customs broker trade data:**
- Commodity description (must be specific — "general merchandise" is not acceptable)
- Number of pieces and unit of measure
- Gross weight in kilograms
- Country of origin
- Harmonized System (HS) tariff code at minimum 6-digit level
- Value for duty

### Filing Deadlines

For highway shipments, the ACI eManifest must be filed **at least 1 hour before arrival** at the Canadian port of entry. "Arrival" means the moment you enter the designated border crossing facility — not when you approach the city or reach the staging area.

In practice, you should file your ACI manifest at your staging stop, which is typically 30–60 km from the crossing. Filing from the staging area gives you time to correct any errors before you are committed to the approach.

**Do not file on the road.** Filing while driving is dangerous, and filing from a phone in a crossing lineup is a bad idea because corrections under time pressure lead to additional errors.

### How to File ACI

CBSA offers three filing channels:

1. **CBSA's eManifest Portal** (direct filing, free, requires a carrier code and web account)
2. **Third-party service provider** (paid service, handles the technical submission on your behalf)
3. **TMS with ACI integration** (your TMS submits the manifest automatically from load data already in the system)

## ACE eManifest: Filing for Entry Into the United States

### What Must Be Filed

CBP's ACE highway manifest requirements closely mirror CBSA's ACI requirements but use US-specific data formats and identifiers.

**Carrier data (your responsibility):**
- US DOT number (required — Canadian carriers operating in the US must obtain a US DOT number and MC number)
- Truck and trailer plate numbers and state/province of registration
- Driver information: full legal name, date of birth, citizenship, driver's licence number and state/province, passport or travel document number

**Cargo data:**
- Shipper name and address (US or Canadian)
- Consignee name and address
- Commodity description (specific — CBP is stricter than CBSA on vague commodity descriptions)
- Number of packages and unit of measure
- Gross weight in kilograms or pounds
- Country of origin
- Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS) code (US-specific code set, different from the HS codes used in Canada)
- Value in US dollars

### Filing Deadlines

ACE highway manifests must be filed **at least 1 hour before arrival** at a US port of entry. The same staging-stop filing discipline applies.

### How to File ACE

CBP offers filing through the ACE portal, through customs brokers (who handle the ABI — Automated Broker Interface — side of the filing), and through TMS integrations. Most Canadian carriers use a US customs broker for ACE filings because the broker also handles the formal entry on the US side. The carrier's responsibility is to ensure the carrier pre-arrival data is filed on time; the broker handles the trade data submission.

## Required Data You Need Before You Can File

Trying to file an eManifest without complete information is the most common cause of errors and delays. Before you approach any border crossing with a loaded trailer, you need the following documents confirmed and in hand.

**Commercial invoice or bill of lading** with:
- Specific commodity description (avoid abbreviations that customs systems may not recognize)
- Verified gross weight — use certified scale weights if available, not driver estimates
- Number of pieces and units of measure matching the physical load
- Shipper and consignee information exactly as they appear in the customs entry

**HS / HTS codes:** These must match the actual commodity. If you are moving a mixed load with multiple commodity types, each commodity requires its own code. Your customs broker can confirm codes if you are unsure — do not guess.

**Shipper and consignee identifiers:** For ACI, CBSA may require the Business Number (BN) of the Canadian importer. For ACE, CBP uses the EIN (Employer Identification Number) of the US importer. Your customs broker will have these on file for regular trading partners.

## The 8 Most Common eManifest Errors

**1. Vague commodity descriptions.** "Freight" "goods" "merchandise" or "auto parts" are not acceptable descriptions. CBSA and CBP both require descriptions specific enough to determine the applicable tariff rate and admissibility. "Automotive gasket assemblies" or "polyethylene packaging film on rolls" are the level of specificity required.

**2. Weight discrepancies.** If your manifest weight and the weight on the bill of lading differ by more than a few percentage points, expect a secondary examination. Always use the same weight source across all your documents.

**3. Wrong trailer number.** Equipment swaps, rebooked loads, and split trailers create situations where the trailer number on the manifest doesn't match the physical trailer at the crossing. Validate your trailer number at the time of filing, not when you first planned the trip.

**4. Late filing.** Filing your ACI or ACE manifest with less than 1 hour before arrival at the port of entry triggers an automatic late-filing flag. Even if no error has occurred, a late filing can result in additional inspection and an Administrative Monetary Penalty.

**5. Not updating the manifest after a load change.** If a load is added to, reduced, or re-routed after the manifest has been filed, the manifest must be amended before you arrive. Filing a manifest and then changing the load without amending is treated as a misrepresentation.

**6. Driver data errors.** Middle names, name order variations, and document numbers must match the physical document exactly. A manifest with "John A. Smith" when the FAST card reads "John Alan Smith" can cause a secondary referral.

**7. Not having a response from CBSA or CBP before approaching the crossing.** After filing your ACI manifest, CBSA sends a response — either a Hold (DO NOT PROCEED) or a green indicator. You must have received a response and it must not be a Hold before you approach the crossing. Filing and immediately driving to the crossing without waiting for the response is a common mistake among newer cross-border operators.

**8. Using the wrong port code.** CBSA and CBP both use specific numeric codes for each port of entry. Filing your manifest with the code for the Peace Bridge when you intend to cross at the Blue Water Bridge means your manifest is filed for the wrong crossing. Always confirm the port code before filing.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

CBSA's Administrative Monetary Penalties (AMPs) for ACI eManifest violations are structured by violation type and history.

| Violation | First Occurrence | Subsequent |
|---|---|---|
| Failure to provide advance information | $2,000 | $8,000 |
| Failure to provide accurate information | $2,000 | $8,000 |
| Late filing (under 1 hour) | $1,000 | $4,000 |
| Failure to amend manifest after load change | $1,500 | $6,000 |

Carriers who accumulate sufficient AMPs face suspension from the ACI program, which effectively means they cannot cross into Canada with commercial loads until the suspension is resolved.

CBP penalties for ACE violations can include civil monetary penalties per shipment and, for persistent non-compliance, suspension of the carrier's operating authority in the United States.

## Best Practices for Carriers Who Cross Regularly

Cross-border carriers who process multiple manifests per week benefit from systematizing the process rather than treating each filing as a one-off task.

**Create manifest templates for recurring lanes.** If you regularly run a Ford Motor Company supplier from Windsor to a Michigan assembly plant, the commodity descriptions, HS codes, shipper, and consignee are the same every trip. Create a saved template and update only the date, driver, equipment, and weight for each load.

**Build a pre-crossing checklist into your dispatch workflow.** Every load that will cross the border should go through the same checklist: manifest filed, broker notified, driver paperwork confirmed, equipment numbers validated. A checklist in your TMS that a dispatcher completes before releasing the load prevents the "I thought the driver filed it" miscommunication.

**Keep your carrier codes and DOT number current.** CBSA carrier codes and US DOT/MC numbers expire or require updates when your company information changes. A manifest filed under an expired or inaccurate carrier code will be rejected or flagged.

**Train your drivers on the response process.** Every driver crossing the border should know how to access the ACI/ACE response for their manifest, recognize a Hold status, and know what to do if they receive one (stop, do not approach the crossing, contact dispatch immediately).

Cross-border trucking is one of the most compliance-intensive activities in the Canadian freight industry. Carriers who invest in understanding and systematizing their eManifest process reduce delays, avoid penalties, and build the consistent performance record that supports FAST approval and favorable inspection rates at the border.
