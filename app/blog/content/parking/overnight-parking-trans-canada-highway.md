## The Trans-Canada: Canada's Longest Freight Corridor

The Trans-Canada Highway is 7,821 kilometres long, stretching from Victoria, British Columbia to St. John's, Newfoundland. For long-haul truckers, it is the backbone of pan-Canadian freight movement — and one of the most demanding planning challenges in the country for overnight parking.

The TCH's character changes dramatically from one region to the next. In southern BC and the Fraser Valley, it is a multi-lane controlled access highway surrounded by urban freight infrastructure. Through the Rockies, it is a mountain highway with steep grades and limited services. Across the Prairies it becomes flat, fast, and sparse. Through Ontario and Quebec it passes through the densest freight corridors in Canada. In the Maritimes and Atlantic Canada it narrows to two lanes with long gaps between services.

Planning your overnight stops correctly on the Trans-Canada requires knowing what the road is like at your planned stop point — not just the average distance between cities. This guide covers the TCH province by province from west to east.

## British Columbia: Lower Mainland to the Alberta Border

The BC section of the Trans-Canada (Highway 1) begins at the Vancouver-area ferry terminals and runs through the Fraser Valley, the mountains, and the interior to the Alberta border at Field/Banff.

**Lower Mainland (Vancouver to Hope):**
The Fraser Valley between the Lower Mainland and Hope has the best-developed commercial parking infrastructure on the BC section. Langley, Abbotsford, and Chilliwack all have private truck parking facilities along the Highway 1 corridor. This 150-km section has the most options per kilometre of any stretch in BC.

Key locations: Langley (24 km from Surrey); Abbotsford (70 km); Chilliwack (100 km). All three have facilities with full amenities.

**The Canyon and Interior (Hope to Kamloops):**
Hope marks the transition from the valley to the mountain section. The route through the Fraser Canyon (Highway 1) is dramatic and narrow in places — not suitable for wide loads. Many long-haul drivers use the Coquihalla Highway (Highway 5) as a faster alternative between Hope and Kamloops, then reconnect with the TCH at Kamloops.

Kamloops is the key overnight stop for the BC interior. The city has multiple private facilities along the Trans-Canada Business Route and along Highway 5A south of the city. Kamloops is approximately 350 km from Vancouver — a natural break point for eastbound traffic that loaded in the Lower Mainland in the morning.

**Revelstoke to Golden:**
The mountain section between Revelstoke and Golden passes through Rogers Pass — one of the most challenging mountain drives in Canada, subject to avalanche control closures and extreme winter conditions. The rest area at Rogers Pass Summit is minimal; plan your overnight stop at Revelstoke (320 km east of Kamloops) or Golden (150 km further east). Both have private facilities, fuel, and 24-hour services.

**Note on Roger's Pass winter operations:** During winter, Rogers Pass operations are managed by Avalanche Canada and Transport Canada jointly. The pass closes frequently for avalanche blasting — closures can last 2–6 hours. Do not plan to transit Rogers Pass at night in winter without checking the current status first.

## Alberta: Field to Saskatchewan Border

Alberta's section of the Trans-Canada runs from the BC border at Field through Banff, Calgary, and Medicine Hat to the Saskatchewan border.

**Banff and the mountain parks:**
Commercial vehicle operations in Banff National Park require a park permit (commercial vehicle fee). Overnight parking at the Banff townsite is restricted. Plan to stage east of the park — Lake Louise (55 km east of Field) is the last service point before entering Alberta proper. Canmore (105 km from Field) is the first full-service stop in Alberta and has private facilities.

**Calgary:**
Calgary is the major overnight hub on the Alberta TCH. The city has extensive private parking infrastructure in its east and south industrial zones, particularly along the Trans-Canada corridor through Stoney Trail and 84th Street. Calgary facilities range from basic to full-service and are generally available without advance booking except during peak Stampede week in July.

Distances from Calgary: Banff/Canmore is 100–120 km west; Medicine Hat is 290 km east.

**Medicine Hat:**
Medicine Hat is the natural overnight point between Calgary and the Saskatchewan border. The Trans-Canada through Medicine Hat has several private facilities and a large Flying J/Pilot-affiliated stop. Medicine Hat is approximately 290 km from Calgary and 185 km from Swift Current, Saskatchewan.

## Saskatchewan: Border to Manitoba

Saskatchewan's section of the TCH (Highway 1) runs 630 km from the Alberta border to the Manitoba border near Moosomin. The Prairie sections are flat and fast but have very limited parking infrastructure outside the main cities.

**Swift Current:**
Swift Current is the primary staging city on the Saskatchewan TCH, approximately 245 km east of the Alberta border. The Trans-Canada Business Route through Swift Current has private facilities and fuel. This is a reliable overnight point.

**Moose Jaw and Regina:**
Moose Jaw (190 km east of Swift Current) and Regina (65 km further east) both have private truck parking. The Pilot Butte area east of Regina is well-regarded and offers better availability than in-city Regina facilities. Regina is approximately 510 km from the Alberta border — a full day's drive for an eastbound truck.

**Rural sections:**
Between Swift Current and Moose Jaw, and between Regina and the Manitoba border, public rest areas are the main option. These are maintained by Saskatchewan Highways and are generally clean with adequate space. They have no amenities beyond washrooms. Plan fuel stops at the cities — rural fuel availability is limited.

## Manitoba: Border to Ontario

Manitoba's section of the TCH (Highway 1) runs 460 km from Saskatchewan to the Ontario border at Falcon Lake.

**Winnipeg:**
Winnipeg is the major hub on the Manitoba TCH. Inkster Industrial Park and the Panet Road area in north Winnipeg have the most established truck parking facilities. Winnipeg is approximately 340 km from the Alberta/Saskatchewan border at the TCH, though most drivers measure from Regina (~475 km from Winnipeg).

Winnipeg facilities are well-developed, with full-service operations, fuel, and both short-term and overnight parking. Book ahead in Winnipeg — it is a major distribution hub and facilities fill regularly.

**Portage la Prairie:**
Portage la Prairie, 85 km west of Winnipeg, serves as an alternative staging point for drivers who want to avoid the city or who have more hours available. The TCH through Portage has fuel and a handful of private lot options. Smaller and less developed than Winnipeg, but functional.

**Brandon:**
Brandon, 215 km west of Winnipeg, is a practical midpoint between Winnipeg and the Alberta border. The Trans-Canada through Brandon has private parking and full fuel services. Brandon is less congested than Winnipeg and frequently has available spots when Winnipeg facilities are full.

**East of Winnipeg (toward Ontario):**
The stretch from Winnipeg to the Ontario border at Falcon Lake (180 km) is sparsely serviced. Kenora, just inside Ontario, is the first full-service point. Plan accordingly — don't leave Winnipeg without confirming your fuel level and parking plan for this section.

## Ontario: Manitoba Border to Quebec

Ontario's Trans-Canada is complex because the highway follows two distinct paths: the northern route through Sudbury, Sault Ste. Marie, and Thunder Bay; and the southern route along the 401 corridor through Toronto and into Montreal. Most long-haul drivers split these depending on their destination.

**Northern TCH (Highway 17/Trans-Canada through northern Ontario):**
Northern Ontario is the most challenging section of the TCH for parking. The distances between services are long, and the communities are small. Key overnight stops:
- Kenora (80 km east of Manitoba border) — fuel, basic facilities
- Dryden (210 km from Manitoba border) — private facilities, fuel
- Thunder Bay (480 km from Manitoba border) — major city, full services, multiple private facilities
- Wawa (750 km) — basic services, government rest area
- Sault Ste. Marie (950 km) — full-service city, multiple private lots
- Sudbury (1,200 km from Manitoba border) — major hub with complete parking infrastructure

The Northern TCH section between Wawa and Sault Ste. Marie (approximately 230 km of highway through Ontario's northern bush) is the most isolated section of the TCH in Ontario. Plan your fuel and parking carefully.

**Southern route (Highway 401):**
Drivers routed through the 401 via Toronto have far more options — detailed in our [Truck Parking Near Toronto guide](/blog/truck-parking-near-toronto). The 401 is well-served with private facilities from Windsor through to the Quebec border at Vaudreuil.

## Quebec: Ontario Border to New Brunswick

Quebec's TCH section follows Autoroute 40 and 20 through Montreal and east to Rivière-du-Loup, where it connects to the Maritime route. This corridor is covered in detail in our Montreal and Quebec City parking guide.

**Rivière-du-Loup** (320 km east of Quebec City) is an important stop — it is the junction point for traffic heading south to New Brunswick or continuing along the south shore of the St. Lawrence. The city has truck parking facilities and is a reliable rest point for drivers running the Maritime route.

## New Brunswick: Quebec Border to Nova Scotia

New Brunswick's section of the TCH (Highway 2) runs 513 km from the Quebec border near Edmundston to the Nova Scotia border near Amherst.

**Edmundston:** Near the Quebec border, Edmundston has fuel and basic private parking. A natural first stop for eastbound traffic entering New Brunswick.

**Fredericton:** At the midpoint of New Brunswick, Fredericton has multiple private facilities along the Trans-Canada. The city's industrial zone off the Regent Street and Ring Road exits has the most parking options.

**Moncton:** Moncton is the distribution hub of the Maritimes. The Trans-Canada through the Mapleton Road and Wheeler Boulevard corridor has the most truck parking infrastructure in Atlantic Canada east of Quebec. For drivers running Atlantic Canada routes, Moncton is the standard hub stop.

## Nova Scotia, Prince Edward Island, and Newfoundland

Atlantic Canada's TCH sections are the least congested in Canada, but they are also the most remote from major services.

**Nova Scotia (Highway 104):** The 104 from Amherst to Sydney runs 420 km. Truro (120 km from Amherst) and New Glasgow (250 km) are the primary service stops. Sydney at the eastern tip of Cape Breton has limited private parking but municipal rest areas.

**Newfoundland (TCH from Port aux Basques to St. John's):** The Newfoundland section runs 900 km and is the most isolated stretch of the TCH. Corner Brook (165 km from Port aux Basques) is the first major city with truck services. Gander (650 km from Port aux Basques) is a reliable midpoint. St. John's at the eastern terminus has industrial area parking. Service gaps between Corner Brook and Gander require careful fuel planning.

## Trans-Canada Planning Principles

Three rules apply to planning overnight stops on the TCH regardless of which section you're running.

**Never rely on a single facility.** On isolated TCH sections, if your primary facility is full or closed, your next option may be 150 km away. Always identify your backup before you start the day's drive, not when you're running low on hours.

**Fuel planning and parking planning are connected.** On remote sections, the truck stop that has a full lot is often the same one that has the last diesel for the next 200 km. Don't choose your overnight location only on parking criteria — integrate fuel stop planning so you don't sacrifice one for the other.

**Weather changes everything in the mountains and north.** Trans-Canada travel plans that assume normal driving times fail in winter weather events. On the BC mountain section, the Alberta mountain corridor, northern Ontario, and Newfoundland, add contingency time to your schedule and have flexible parking options identified at multiple points rather than a single fixed destination.
