## The Scale of the Problem: Canada's Truck Parking Deficit in 2026

Canada's truck parking shortage has crossed from inconvenience into infrastructure crisis. The Canadian Trucking Alliance estimates a national deficit of approximately 10,000 to 12,000 commercial truck parking spaces, with the gap widening every year as freight volumes grow faster than infrastructure investment. In 2026, that deficit is concentrated most severely in three corridors: the Highway 401 Golden Horseshoe region between Toronto and Windsor, the Lower Mainland of British Columbia surrounding Metro Vancouver and the Port of Vancouver, and the Autoroute 20/40 industrial corridor west of Montreal.

The numbers behind the shortage are stark. According to Transport Canada's 2025 Commercial Vehicle Survey, the number of licensed Class 8 commercial vehicles operating in Canada grew by 8.4% between 2022 and 2025. Over the same period, the total inventory of dedicated commercial truck parking spaces increased by less than 2%. The gap between supply and demand is not closing — it is accelerating.

Industry research conducted by the Ontario Trucking Association in 2025 found that 76% of Ontario-based long-haul drivers reported difficulty finding legal parking at least once per week. Among drivers operating the Highway 401 corridor between Toronto and the Quebec border, that figure rose to 89%. Drivers are not occasionally inconvenienced — they are routinely forced to choose between parking illegally, driving beyond their legal hours, or pulling into locations that create genuine safety and cargo security risks.

## Why the Shortage Is Getting Worse

The truck parking shortage is not a new problem, but several converging forces are making it significantly worse in 2026 than it was five years ago.

**E-commerce freight growth** has increased total truck movements while compressing delivery windows. The rise of same-day and next-day consumer delivery services has pushed more trucks into urban and suburban freight corridors, precisely where parking capacity is lowest. A truck that once made two or three trips per week to a distribution centre may now make daily runs, increasing the frequency with which drivers need overnight parking in constrained urban-adjacent locations.

**ELD enforcement** has eliminated the buffer that drivers once had when parking was difficult. Before mandatory electronic logging devices for federally regulated carriers (required since January 2023), a driver who was 20 minutes past their HOS limit in a parking lot hunt had some practical ability to self-report differently. ELD devices record location and duty status automatically. A driver whose HOS clock expires at 9:47 PM in the Brampton industrial corridor now has a timestamped violation on their record, regardless of whether they had a legal parking alternative available. This has increased the urgency and visibility of the shortage.

**Industrial land values** in major Canadian freight markets have eliminated many of the informal parking areas that drivers relied on for decades. In Surrey, BC, land that hosted a gravel truck parking lot in 2015 is now a warehouse distribution centre. In Mississauga and Brampton, industrial parcels that once had surplus yard space usable for overnight parking have been built out to their maximum coverage. The informal safety valve of cheap, available land near major freight nodes is gone in most primary markets.

**Municipal opposition to new facilities** has stalled many private-sector efforts to add parking supply. Truck parking facilities generate noise, lighting, and traffic — all of which generate complaints from adjacent residential and commercial property owners. Rezoning applications for new truck parking in Ontario's Peel Region have faced significant local opposition, and several proposed facilities have been denied or deferred at the municipal planning stage.

## Government Response: What Has Been Done and What Hasn't

Federal and provincial governments have acknowledged the truck parking shortage publicly, but tangible infrastructure investment has been limited.

Transport Canada's 2024 National Trade Corridors Fund allocated funding for several infrastructure projects, but dedicated truck parking was not a priority category. Rest area improvements under provincial highway programs — notably the Ministry of Transportation Ontario's Highway Improvement Program — have added capacity at some 400-series locations, but the additions have been incremental rather than transformative.

The most substantive government response has come in the form of study and consultation rather than construction. The Canadian Council of Motor Transport Administrators (CCMTA) released a National Truck Parking Study in 2025 that documented the shortage comprehensively and made recommendations for a federal-provincial cost-sharing framework to fund dedicated truck parking facilities at strategic points on the National Highway System. As of early 2026, that framework has not been funded.

Ontario's government released a Freight Supportive Guidelines update in 2025 that encouraged municipalities to consider truck parking needs in official plan amendments and zoning bylaws. The guidelines are advisory only — municipalities are not required to approve truck parking applications — and early implementation has been inconsistent across the province.

British Columbia's Ministry of Transportation completed a rest area capacity review in 2024 covering the Trans-Canada through the Lower Mainland and Interior. Several rest area expansions were identified as priority projects, with the Merritt and Hope areas flagged for near-term expansion. Construction timelines remain contingent on capital budget allocations.

## Technology Solutions Filling the Gap

In the absence of adequate physical infrastructure, technology platforms have emerged to make the most of existing parking capacity.

Parking reservation platforms — including the Truck Parking Club at parking.truckerpro.ca — aggregate private and commercial parking inventory, allow real-time availability checks, and enable advance booking. For drivers and fleets, this does not create new parking spaces, but it dramatically reduces the time spent searching for available spots and allows route planning to incorporate confirmed parking rather than best guesses.

Dynamic parking guidance systems, already common at major European freight terminals and US truck stops, are beginning to appear at larger Canadian facilities. Digital signs showing available space counts at approaching facilities allow drivers to make diversion decisions before they commit to a full exit sequence. Several Ontario Ministry of Transportation pilot projects along the 400 series are testing variable-message signs with truck parking availability data.

Trailer management and drop-and-hook optimization can reduce the duration a given tractor-trailer combination occupies a parking space by improving turnaround efficiency. A fleet that moves from live-load operations to drop-and-hook at shipper facilities reduces the time drivers spend waiting — and waiting trucks take up parking spaces. Operational efficiency and parking capacity are directly linked.

## Municipal Opposition and Land Use Conflict

The private-sector response to the parking shortage — build more facilities — runs directly into municipal land use opposition in most high-demand markets.

Truck parking facilities are classified as a transportation use under most municipal official plans, and they are typically permitted in industrial zones. But the permitting process creates significant friction. Rezoning applications require public consultation, and in markets where industrial land is adjacent to residential areas — which describes much of Peel Region and Metro Vancouver — neighbourhood opposition can delay or defeat applications regardless of their merits.

The economic case for private truck parking is also challenging. Truck parking generates revenue of $15 to $40 per space per night, which must cover land costs, construction, fencing, lighting, CCTV infrastructure, insurance, and ongoing maintenance. In markets where industrial land costs $3 million to $6 million per acre (as in parts of the Greater Toronto Area and Lower Mainland), the economics of a standalone truck parking facility rarely work without a very long time horizon or ancillary revenue from fuel, food service, or maintenance.

This economic reality has concentrated private parking investment in secondary markets where land costs are lower — areas like Barrie and Woodstock in Ontario, Kamloops and Kelowna in BC, and Red Deer in Alberta — rather than in the highest-demand zones immediately adjacent to major urban centres.

## Projected Growth: Where This Is Heading by 2030

The structural factors driving the shortage are not reversing. E-commerce freight growth is projected to continue at 6–8% annually through 2030. The federal push to increase Canadian export capacity, particularly in the agriculture and energy sectors, will add freight volume in western Canadian corridors. Port of Vancouver expansion projects currently underway will increase container throughput significantly, adding to Lower Mainland truck movements.

The Canadian Trucking Alliance projects that without targeted investment — in public rest area expansion, private facility development incentives, and municipal land use policy reform — the national parking deficit will reach 15,000 to 18,000 spaces by 2030.

The consequences of that deficit extend beyond driver inconvenience. Parking shortages contribute directly to Hours of Service violations, cargo theft at unsecured locations, fatigue-related accidents, and driver attrition. A trucking industry that cannot accommodate its drivers' basic compliance and safety needs will struggle to recruit the next generation of professional drivers into a profession already facing a significant labour shortage.

The shortage is solvable. It requires coordinated action between federal infrastructure funding, provincial planning policy, and municipal land use decisions — plus continued private-sector investment supported by realistic economics. The question is whether those pieces come together before the deficit becomes a supply chain crisis.
