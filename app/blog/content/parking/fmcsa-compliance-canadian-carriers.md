## Why FMCSA Compliance Matters for Canadian Carriers

The Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration is the US federal agency that regulates commercial motor vehicle safety, and its reach does not stop at the Canadian border. Any Canadian carrier that hauls freight into or through the United States is subject to FMCSA regulations, must register with the agency, and operates under a safety measurement framework that tracks every US inspection and violation — whether or not the carrier's headquarters is in Toronto, Calgary, or Vancouver.

FMCSA compliance is not optional, and it is not a formality. Canadian carriers with poor FMCSA safety records face targeted roadside inspections, operational interventions, and potential suspension of their US operating authority. A carrier that loses its US operating authority has lost access to the most important cross-border freight market in the world. Understanding how the system works — and how to operate within it successfully — is a fundamental competency for any Canadian carrier moving freight across the 49th parallel.

## US Operating Authority: USDOT Number and MC Number

Every Canadian carrier that operates commercial vehicles in the United States must obtain two identifiers from FMCSA: a USDOT number and, for carriers engaged in for-hire interstate commerce, an MC number.

**USDOT Number**

The USDOT number is assigned through FMCSA registration and is the carrier's primary identifier in all US federal safety databases. It appears on the vehicle during US operations. To obtain a USDOT number, Canadian carriers register through FMCSA's Unified Registration System (URS) at fmcsa.dot.gov. The application requires basic carrier information, fleet size, cargo descriptions, and insurance documentation.

USDOT registration is required for:
- All Canadian carriers operating vehicles with a gross vehicle weight rating (GVWR) of 4,536 kg (10,001 lbs) or more in interstate commerce
- Carriers transporting hazardous materials in any quantity requiring placarding
- Carriers transporting passengers for compensation

**MC Number (Motor Carrier Operating Authority)**

For-hire carriers — those transporting goods that belong to others, i.e. not private carriers moving their own goods — must also obtain an MC number, which represents the carrier's operating authority. The MC number authorizes the carrier to conduct for-hire transportation in interstate commerce.

The MC number application requires:
- Proof of insurance at minimum required levels ($750,000 to $5,000,000 in public liability depending on cargo type)
- Filing of a BOC-3 designation (see below)
- A minimum 10-business-day waiting period after application for protests or objections

Private carriers (those hauling only their own goods) require a USDOT number but not an MC number.

## BOC-3: Process Agent Designation

One of the requirements that catches many Canadian carriers off guard is the BOC-3 filing. A BOC-3 is a form that designates a process agent — a US-based legal representative — in every state where the carrier operates or intends to operate.

The process agent receives legal documents, court papers, and regulatory notices on behalf of the carrier when the carrier cannot be served directly. Canadian carriers are required to designate agents in all US states they operate in, which for most cross-border carriers means designating agents in multiple states.

BOC-3 agents can be appointed individually for each state (requiring a separate written agreement with each agent) or through a blanket BOC-3 service provider — companies that offer nationwide agent coverage for a flat annual fee of $30–$75. Virtually all Canadian cross-border carriers use the blanket service approach. Filing is done directly through FMCSA's Licensing and Insurance (L&I) system.

## Insurance Requirements for US Operations

FMCSA mandates minimum insurance levels that exceed most Canadian domestic requirements. The minimums are:

| Cargo Type | Minimum Public Liability |
|---|---|
| Non-hazardous property (general freight, most standard loads) | $750,000 USD |
| Household goods | $750,000 USD |
| Hazardous materials (certain categories) | $1,000,000 USD |
| Hazardous materials (radioactive, explosives, certain dangerous goods) | $5,000,000 USD |

Canadian carriers must file evidence of insurance coverage meeting these minimums directly with FMCSA. Most Canadian commercial trucking insurers offer US liability endorsements — your broker should be familiar with the FMCSA filing requirement. The insurance provider files Form MCS-90 (for public liability) and/or Form MCS-82 (for cargo insurance, if required) directly with FMCSA. Insurance filing is maintained continuously — if coverage lapses, FMCSA automatically deactivates the carrier's operating authority until coverage is reinstated.

## CSA: Compliance, Safety, Accountability — What Canadian Carriers Need to Know

CSA is FMCSA's safety measurement and enforcement framework. Every US roadside inspection of a carrier's vehicles and drivers generates data that is fed into the CSA system. That data determines the carrier's visibility to FMCSA enforcement, the frequency with which their vehicles are selected for inspection at weigh stations and roadside checks, and the likelihood of receiving an FMCSA investigation.

**The Seven BASICs**

CSA organizes safety performance into seven Behavior Analysis and Safety Improvement Categories, universally referred to as BASICs:

1. **Unsafe Driving** — Speeding, reckless driving, improper lane changes, following too closely. This BASIC has the highest weight in CSA scoring because of its direct accident causation link.

2. **Hours of Service Compliance** — HOS violations recorded by officers during inspections, including missing or incomplete logbooks (or ELD malfunctions), exceeding driving or on-duty time limits.

3. **Driver Fitness** — Invalid CDL (or equivalent Canadian licence), medical certificate violations, disqualified drivers.

4. **Controlled Substances and Alcohol** — Drug and alcohol violations found during inspections or post-accident testing.

5. **Vehicle Maintenance** — Brake defects, lighting violations, tire conditions, coupling system defects. This BASIC generates the most violations for Canadian carriers in FMCSA data because maintenance standards differ slightly and inspection officers apply FMCSA-standard criteria regardless of provincial certification.

6. **Hazardous Materials Compliance** — Placarding, shipping paper, packaging, and handling violations for carriers moving dangerous goods.

7. **Crash Indicator** — A measure derived from a carrier's US accident history. Unlike the other BASICs, this one cannot be improved by passing inspections — only by avoiding accidents.

**How CSA Scores Work**

For each BASIC, FMCSA calculates a percentile score comparing the carrier to similar carriers (similar size, similar miles). Scores above certain thresholds trigger different levels of FMCSA attention:

- **Scores above 65th percentile:** The BASIC appears in the carrier's public SMS profile. Shippers can see it.
- **Scores above 80th percentile (Alert status):** FMCSA may initiate a warning letter, an offsite investigation, or enhanced roadside targeting of the carrier's vehicles.
- **Scores above 90th percentile:** High likelihood of a full FMCSA compliance review.

Canadian carriers can monitor their CSA scores through FMCSA's Safety Measurement System portal at safer.fmcsa.dot.gov, or through third-party safety monitoring services. The [FMCSA lookup at truckerpro.net](https://truckerpro.net) provides quick access to carrier safety records and SMS data.

## US Hours of Service: Key Differences from Transport Canada Rules

Canadian HOS rules and US HOS rules are similar in structure but different in important details. Drivers switching between Canadian and US operations must understand which set of rules applies at any given time — and FMCSA inspectors apply US rules to all drivers operating within the United States, regardless of carrier nationality.

**Key differences for Canadian drivers in the US:**

| Parameter | Transport Canada | US FMCSA |
|---|---|---|
| Off-duty restart requirement | 8 consecutive hours | 10 consecutive hours |
| Maximum driving per shift | 13 hours | 11 hours |
| Maximum on-duty per shift | 14 hours | 14 hours |
| Weekly cycle option A | 70 hours / 7 days | 60 hours / 7 days |
| Weekly cycle option B | 120 hours / 14 days | 70 hours / 8 days |
| Sleeper berth provision | 2+6 split allowed | 7+3 split allowed (post-2020 rule) |
| 30-minute break requirement | Not required | Required after 8 hours driving |

The most operationally significant difference for most Canadian carriers is the required 10-hour off-duty period (vs. 8 hours in Canada) and the 11-hour maximum driving time (vs. 13 in Canada). Drivers who plan their US trips based on Canadian HOS parameters will find themselves out of hours before their planned stops.

ELD devices used by Canadian carriers operating in the US must be FMCSA-registered. Canadian-certified ELDs that are not on FMCSA's registered device list are not compliant for US operations, even if the device is Transport Canada-compliant. Verify your device's FMCSA registration status before operating south of the border.

## DOT Roadside Inspections: What to Expect

The Commercial Vehicle Safety Alliance (CVSA) administers the North American Standard inspection program used by both Canadian and US enforcement officers. When a CVSA inspection occurs in the United States, the results — violations found, driver and vehicle out-of-service conditions — are recorded in FMCSA's DataQs system and contribute to the carrier's CSA score.

**Inspection levels:** CVSA inspections range from Level I (full inspection of both driver and vehicle, the most comprehensive) through Level VI (enhanced NAS inspection for radioactive shipments). Level I inspections are the most common and examine driver credentials, HOS compliance, and vehicle mechanical condition thoroughly. Level II inspections focus on driver credentials and visible vehicle components without crawling under the vehicle.

**Out-of-service conditions:** If an inspection officer finds a violation severe enough to take the driver or vehicle out of service (OOS), the vehicle cannot move until the violation is corrected. Common OOS conditions for Canadian carriers include brake deficiencies (brake adjustment is the most common OOS violation for Canadian trucks at US inspections), lighting defects, and HOS violations. An OOS condition generates a serious CSA violation that significantly affects the relevant BASIC score.

**Pre-trip preparation for US operations:** Ensure your vehicles meet FMCSA brake adjustment standards (which differ slightly from Canadian NSS standards in measurement methodology), that all lighting is fully functional, that tires meet US minimum tread depth requirements (4/32" on steer axles, 2/32" on drive and trailer axles), and that coupling devices are in proper condition. A vehicle that passes Canadian pre-trip standards will generally pass US inspection standards, but specific items — particularly brake adjustment — benefit from verification against the FMCSA-standard measurement criteria.

## Carrier Safety Ratings and What They Mean

FMCSA assigns safety ratings to carriers following compliance reviews. The rating categories are:

- **Satisfactory:** The carrier's safety management controls are adequate.
- **Conditional:** Safety management deficiencies exist. A conditional carrier is operating legally but may face additional enforcement scrutiny.
- **Unsatisfactory:** The carrier has demonstrated a pattern of non-compliance. An unsatisfactory rating prohibits the carrier from operating in the US until the rating is improved through a successful compliance review.

New Canadian carriers registering with FMCSA do not receive a formal safety rating initially — they operate on a "not rated" basis, which is acceptable for normal operations. A formal safety rating is only assigned following a compliance review, which FMCSA initiates based on CSA score thresholds, specific incidents, or routine audit selection.

Maintaining a satisfactory safety rating — or operating in a way that would merit one if reviewed — requires consistent attention to driver qualification files, HOS compliance, vehicle maintenance records, and drug and alcohol testing program compliance. For Canadian carriers not familiar with FMCSA's record-keeping requirements, the qualification file standards in particular (medical examiner's certificates, road test certifications, pre-employment inquiries to previous employers) differ from Transport Canada requirements and are a frequent source of non-compliance for carriers transitioning to US operations.
